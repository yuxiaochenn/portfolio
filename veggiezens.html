Background Story

As Chinese international students in the States, the struggle of accessing to a wide range of fresh vegetables for Chinese style cooking is real. Not every neighborhood has a grocery place where we can buy fresh vegetables to make of the taste of traditional Chinese dishes - the taste missed by our Chinese stomach. Under this circumstance, most of us have to stick to a relatively limited range of vegetables available in the nearby grocery stores, or commute to Asian or Chinese grocery stores for a larger vegetable selection, which can be costly both money-wise and time-wise.

Initially, I was also one of the group who couldn't pull myself out of the struggle. One day when I was browsing Xia Chu Fang mobile App, a Chinese App aiming to enrich home cooking, I was attracted by an article titled with "The Hidden Gems in North American Grocery Stores You Must Try Next Time." In the article, the author introduced several vegetables that I knew nothing about. The article also listed multiple recipes, accordingly, to demonstrate how those vegetables are great substitutes of some of the Chinese vegetables that are seldomly sold in majority of local grocery stores. Because of my exploded curiosity as a vegetable lover, I took that advice to try those unknown vegetables. From then on, my selection of vegetables in my fridge expanded, as well as my creased interest in health lifestyle, nutrition facts, and clean eating.

Design Solution

By designing and creating this 2D platform game - Veggiezens, I hope to use the art of game and immersive storytelling to help my Chinese peers, who live in the States, to expand their knowledge in the vegetables in their local grocery stores, enrich their home cooking options, and also encourage them to constantly explore unknowns.

Learning Outcomes

Knowledge Level

[Chinese living in the States] Identify unfamiliar vegetable options that are more accessible in local grocery stores in the States, and potential recipes to expand Chinese style home cooking.

[Chinese living in the States] Recognize the health benefits and nutritional facts of intaking various vegetables.

Application Level

[Chinese living in the States] Apply the cooking ideas in the game to real life home cooking.

The Plot

One day, you (the Player) received an SOS letter from the Veggie Planet, which informed you they need your help to rescue four of their Veggiezens kidnapped by their enemies from the Greasssy Planet. You are ready to fight with the evils and save your Veggiezen friends!

Game Structure

Characters Design

Player | A skinny, ordinary human being from the Earth

Rationale: I emphasized that the human being is skinny, because I would like to show a positive change in his physical build after he defeated the evils and rescued the Veggiezens. From a real life perspective, I would like to imply that eating vegetables can bring people healthy benefits.

Four Veggiezens to be Rescured | Turnip, Leek, Rhubarb, and Bokchoy

Rationale: I picked these vegetables and sculpted the Veggiezen characters accordingly. These four vegetables are not highly recognized and widely consumed by Chinese, but they have great nutritional values, as well as the potential to enrich their Chinese style home cooking in the States. They are the great substitutes of four vegetables ingredients in Chinese cooking - Chinese Radish, Shanghai Bokchoy, Chive, and hawthorn.

Four Enemies to be Defeated | Instant Noodle (King of Processed Food), Boba Tea (King of Sugar), Pickle (King of Salt), and Chinese Donut (King of Fried Breakfast Food)

Rationale: By choosing these foods as the original model for the enemies, I would like to inform my Chinese peers how unhealthy these frequently-consumed foods are.

Key Scenes

Opening Scene

Storytelling, Introducing Characters, Setting Game Goal

Tutorial Scene

Game Scene | Fighting with the Enemies

Before rescuing each of the four Veggiezens, the player needs to defeat one evil from the Greasssy Planet.

Game Scene | Rescuing Veggiezens

Each time when successfully rescues a Veggiezen, the player will get into a information section. During the session, the player will learn about the enemy he/she just defeated, as well as the Veggiezen just got rescued.

Quiz Scene

As a follow-up session, the player will be given a multiple choice quiz question based off the vegetable described in the previous information session.

Reward Scene

If the player answers the question correctly, he/she will be rewarded by gaining a nutrition power from the Veggiezen.

If the player doesn't choose the correct answer, no reward will be given. Instead, the Veggiezen will briefly repeat the content corresponding to the correct answer to the question.

Ending Scene

The game and the whole story will be wrapped up with the plot that the player gets the ultimate reward from the Veggie King - a book introducing all the Veggiezens from the Veggie Planet.

The User-Centered Game Design Process

User Research

In order to make sure this is a real user problem, and further understand people's general experience with doing grocery shopping for vegetables, I sent out a simple survey. I collected 12 valid responses within one day.

Key Insights

-100% participants purchase vegetables for home cooking on a regular basis.

-100% participants ranked Chinese dishes as their most frequent home cooking option.

[Translated] "I usually purchase this vegetable. It has very similar taste and texture with Xi Hu lu, so I know it's ideal for Chinese style dishes."

[Translated] "I have a habit of more or less twisting the Western style recipes to better fit my appetite. Even when I cook 'Western style dishes,' I feel most of them are still Chinese dishes with some Western style ingredients."

-Nutrition is an important consideration that influences participants' vegetable purchase; however, they lack the confidence in the vegetable nutritional facts (as shown in the image below).

[Translated] "I prefer cooking with low amount of oil and salt to best lock the nutrition and tastes of the vegetables."

-Most of the participants(11 out of 12) said that they limited the number of their frequently purchased vegetables under 5.

[Translated] "I only stick to the same variety of vegetables when I do grocery shopping each week for my fixed home cooking recipes."

[Translated] "More often, I will pick the ones(vegetables) I have tired before. It's hard to choose the unfamiliar ones, because I don't know any alternatives to cook them."

Persona

Concept Testing

Initial Game Structure

I run 4 concept testing sections with potential users. I explained the flow of the game structure, while showing them the flow chart, and the some key sketches of the game scenes. I also briefly discussed the story plot, the characters, as well as how their rationales point to the learning objectives of the game.

Feedback Highlights

-100% of participants questioned about the opening format of the game.

[Translated] "By only looking at the game structure, I feel the game will start very suddenly."

[Translated] "Will there be a menu at the beginning to inform the player to start the game? That's what I usually see in a platformer game."

-100% of participants questioned about the the interactions happening during the information sessions.

[Translated] "I get you that the player will be required to go through a information session to get educated, but then what? Will they get anything from the sessions? I feel there's a gap here."

[Translated] "Will there be a menu at the beginning to inform the player to start the game? That's what I usually see in a platformer game."

Improved Game Structure

Usability Testing

As a wrap-up of this project, I run five usability test sections to evaluate the final version of the game.

Pre-Test Sessions

During each of the pre-test sessions, I showed the participant a list of vegetable pictures, and asked them to choose the ones they have knowledge in. The four vegetables in the game were included in the list. As a result, none of them were marked by any participants as "recognized."

Game Testing Sessions

All the participants were invited to run a complete cycle of the game on my laptop. While sitting right next to them, with a comfortable distance, for taking notes on their reactions, I used Lookback to record their screens as well as audio, with their permission. As a result, 100% participants successfully finished the game.

Post-Test Sessions

Firstly, I asked them to describe their gaming experience as a whole, their rating of the immersive level during the game, their favorite and least favorite aspects of the game.

Right after their briefings, I showed them the list of vegetables I used for pre-test sessions again, and asked them to re-mark the ones they knew. All participants 100% recognized the four vegetables in the game, and some of the participants also stated one or two things about the vegetables. Two participants showed particularly great interest in learning about more vegetables, and asked me to share with them recipes that I personally cooked vegetables.

Potential Improvements

-All the participants mentioned that the repetitive wording for the Veggiezens' introductions reduced the immersive level of the game. On top of that, 3 out of 5 participants suggested me to make a version in Chinese. In this version, I have Chinese annotations for the key words, but some participants found them to be incohesive with the rest of the English texts.

[Translated] "Staring at those Chinese made me want to skip reading the English contents...You know, the power of mother language."

-Another majoy finding is that most participants would like to see more personalities developed for the characters,especially for each of the Veggiezens.

[Translated] "I really appreciate the rich contents, and the beautifully crafted graphic assets you generated for this game. Since it's a game, a form of art, I hope to distinguish these vegetable characters more easily through their tones and actions."
