Improving address validation experience for Walmart customers

Shipped · End-to-end experience design · Responsive web · Native apps

My role

UI/UX designer - drove design from concept to launch

Cross-functional team

Product Manager, Content Strategist, Design Researcher, team of Developers (iOS, Android, and Web), and support from design team

Overview

As an ecommerce giant, Walmart.com adopts a robust delivery system as a pillar of the success of business. Leveraging address validation services (AVS), we are able to verify if an input from the customer matches an address on file. This has been an effective approach to avoid post-transaction frictions, such as delivery delays or undelivered orders.

Problem space

Back in early 2020, analytical data indicated we still have a wiggling room. We’ve noticed 4% of adding address attempts resulted in an invalid address during checkout. On top of that, a noteworthy percentage of these invalidations implies a limited capability of the AVS. On the business side, we decided to upgrade our AVS license to raise the bar. To reach an experience parity, I worked with the cross-functional team to identify design opportunities to improve the address validation experience on Walmart.com.

Scope & prioritization

I did a comprehensive audit of all use cases to better understand the scope of this project. I learned there are 4 types of address validation scenarios implemented on the current site, with 80% as return customers and 20% as guests. Design specification wise, all platforms need to be taken care of - responsive web, iOS, and Android.

Collaborated with product and engineer partners, I made a tangible plan to prioritize the design efforts on different use cases. Due to technical limitations, the outdated AVS can only surface out address suggestions when there are exactly 2 results on file. The rest, such as singular suggestion use cases, is buried in low confidence suggestion cases. With more accurate responses honored by the latest AVS, it’s a great opportunity to revamp a trustworthy customer experience with more solid and reliable address suggestions. Therefore, I decided to first dig into the use case with high confidence suggestions to maximize the opportunity for a customer experience 2.0.

The North Star - heuristics evaluation

There wasn’t additional bandwidth to weave research studies into the timeline to gain customer insights on the current design. Fortunately, as address validation is a well-studied domain, I was able to find the North Star to base my critique on. My main references were Jakob Nielson’s usability heuristics and Baymard’s UX benchmark of 60 major ecommerce sites on designing for address validators.

At a high level, address validator is a type of error message. In general, our intent aligned with Jakob Nielson's principle on designing for error cases. The validator intervenes customers on a relevant timing to provide address suggestions to fix their address entry mistakes. After a heuristics evaluation and discussion with research and content strategy partners, I was able to identify the space to bring the current design to the next level.

Current design discrepancies

1. There’s the solution, but no explanations on the problem. We attempt to nudge customers to continue with a suggested address; however, the message on the validator doesn’t provide much context on the cause of this blocker and corresponding consequences of an invalid address.

2. There’s no visibility of the entered address. It’s hard to catch the discrepancies between entered and suggested addresses, without aligning them together for side-by-side comparison.

3. There’s no options to override the validator. Baymard’s UX benchmark suggests, as there’s no guarantee for AVS to be 100% error-less, it's always helpful to allow customers to override the validator and continue with their entered address. Such a treatment is overlooked on the current design. The “cancel” button will bring back the address entry stage, but the same validator will be triggered again with another “save address” attempt.

4. There’s space to bring the overall design to the next level, such as to fix the repetitive CTAs and visual alignment discrepancy.

Ideation

Final solution

Taking all into account, I had another round of design iteration and came up with a solution that got all the stakeholders on board. I proposed to turn the validator from an in-page component to a standalone modal. A modal simplifies the information hierarchy, which really helps to explain the complicated navigation logic between the main and sub checkout flows.

What's next

I worked very closely with our engineering team to write specifications for the designs. I delivered a full stack of annotated design specs with 6 address validation use cases with 2 customer across responsive web and native apps. As a wrap, I contributed to the VQA session and identified several key front-end discrepancies before rolling out the new design. While this feature is being A/B tested, let’s stay tuned for the results and impact :)
